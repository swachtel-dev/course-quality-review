Course Quality Review

How we review a course before students see it.

A three-level review, run by AI against our learning-design standards: automated checks, a close read of every unit for whether the economics is correct, and a simulated student walkthrough. It stress-tests its own findings, leaves the instructional designer in control of what changes, and ships the approved fixes live. Here it is on Principles of Macroeconomics.

Prepared for Tom O'Shea 14 units read findings → designer's call → live

The process, in three levels

Each level catches what the one before it can't, ordered from pure measurement to subject-matter reading and human approval. Click any level to see what it checks.

1
Automated checks

Measure what a machine can measure

Structural and workload checks, scored against real data, instant and objective.
▸

Every course is scored against two yardsticks, so nothing here depends on opinion:

A workload benchmark built from real student surveys. Across the catalog, we asked 45,000+ students "how much work is this course versus your others?" The single best predictor of that answer turned out to be how much reading a course puts in front of students, so we measure that and place the course on the curve.

A structural standards check. One clear task per assignment; setup kept short; no more than two graded items a week; required vs. optional clearly marked; long pages that tell you where to look.

And the plain mechanical checks. Every link is followed to confirm it still resolves; every exam is opened to confirm it can actually be graded; the points are summed to confirm they reach the course total. These are the breaks a student hits first and notices most.

What it catches: anything countable, plus dead links and broken math. What it can't: whether the writing actually teaches. That needs reading.
2
An AI reads every unit

Is the economics taught correctly, and honestly?

An AI reads all fourteen units line by line, against our learning-design standards and what's actually true in the field. This is the layer where the substantive judgment lives.
▸

Level 1 says how much; Level 2 asks whether the teaching is right. Each unit is read against our learning-design standards and one hard test: would a careful student leave with a true picture, or with a claim they later have to un-learn?

The questions that take genuine subject-matter judgment. Is a contested view being taught as settled fact? Is a definition subtly wrong? Does the worked example actually prepare a student for what the problem set grades? Is a unit heavy because of its length, or because of unresolved decisions the page never settles?

What it catches: wrong or oversimplified economics, one-sided framing, and gaps between what is taught and what is graded. The next section is what this turned up on Macro.
3
Whole-course experience

Walk it as a student; read it as a designer

Simulate the actual learner journey, then a top-down expert read for arc and coherence.
▸

Checklists miss what only shows up when you move through a course. So at this level we do two reads and keep what they agree on:

A simulated student walkthrough. A learner persona is run through the course start to finish and reports where they get lost, where they wonder what's required, and where it genuinely helps.

A design read. The course is read top to bottom for the through-line across units, whether navigation points to real pages, whether labels are honest, and whether the assessments line up with what's taught.

What it catches: broken navigation, mislabeled material, exams that don't match the teaching, and the experience-level problems no checklist sees. This is the level that proves the review is worth doing.

What that read turned up on Macro, and what shipped

Level 2 is the heart of the review. Here is what the read of Macro's teaching found, how each finding was checked, and what reached the live course. Two safeguards stand between a finding and a single changed word.

1 It raises real economic issues

It asks whether each claim is actually true, and whether a contested view is being taught as settled fact, the kind of thing only a close read of the prose would catch. The read of Macro's teaching raised 83 candidate issues.

2 A skeptic stress-tests each

An independent second pass tries to refute every finding first. It downgraded 42 to minor and rejected one outright, so what reaches the instructional designer is judgment calls, not a pile of nitpicks.

3 The designer decides, then it ships

Each surviving issue is the instructional designer's to approve or reject. 29 were approved and 38 rejected; those 29, plus 15 clear-cut factual fixes, are now live in the course and verified in place.

Four of the changes that shipped (of 44 now live)
Honesty

A contested growth claim, now marked as contested

The course bills itself as honest; its strongest growth claim was stated as near-settled fact.
Before: "The institutions explain almost the entire difference in living standards. This is as close to a controlled experiment as the social sciences get."
After: "Institutions account for much of the difference in living standards. This is about as close to a natural experiment as history offers, though it is a single case and the two Koreas also differed in other ways." The institutions-versus-geography debate is genuinely live; the course now teaches it as a strong view, not a settled fact.
Correctness

An economics error fixed in the first definition students read

GDP was defined in a way the course itself would have to walk back two units later.
Before: GDP is the "total value of all goods and services produced in a country in a year."
After: the "total market value of all final goods and services produced in a country in a year (final, so we do not double-count)." Omitting "final" double-counts intermediate goods; it is the exact simplification a student would have to un-learn when GDP is measured in Unit 8.
Correctness

A unit that quietly contradicted itself

It taught the money multiplier as how banks create money, while saying elsewhere that reserves no longer work that way.
The unit taught that the banking system "has supported several times the original deposit," then separately noted reserves have been abundant since 2008. Both halves can't be the operative story. It now flags the multiplier as a simplified, historical model and states that today bank lending is limited by capital and loan demand, not by a reserve ratio. The student is no longer left to reconcile two contradictory accounts.
New content

A worked example written to fill a graded gap

The problem set graded a skill no worked example actually demonstrated.
Unit 2 grades telling a curve shift apart from a movement along the curve, but every worked example only showed shifts. We wrote one: a coffee frost shifts supply left; the price rise that follows is a movement along the demand curve, not a second shift. The course now demonstrates the exact distinction it tests, so a student who studies the examples is actually prepared for the problem set.
The honest answer to the obvious question
This is where the review earns the word judgment. It makes economic calls, an independent pass filters them, and the instructional designer approves the ones worth making before they ship to the live course. 38 were rejected, including proposed hedges on the minimum wage and on GDP and well-being judged fine for an intro course. The tool proposes; the designer decides.

"The same four-step move taught in Unit 2 (what changed, who's affected, which direction, where the new equilibrium lands) comes back by name at every later scale: a single market, the Fed, aggregate supply and demand, fiscal policy. A student learns one habit of mind and applies it a dozen times."
A checklist can confirm a page loads; only a read can tell you the course has a genuine spine, and that what needed fixing was the scaffolding, not the pedagogy.

The most useful thing the walkthrough surfaced was what carries the course for a nervous student: being told up front that the math is light, paired with an optional self-test to skip what you already know. That is a design pattern worth repeating in every quantitative course, and the kind of thing only a student walkthrough reveals.

Example insights, unit by unit

One finding from each of the fourteen units, in plain terms: what a student would have run into, and what we did about it. Most shipped to the live course; two we looked at and deliberately left alone, which is the human gate doing its job. Shipped live   Declined left as is. Click any unit.

1
Unit 1 · Onboarding: Growth Is Real
A headline number that looked exact but wasn't
Shipped
To make growth vivid, the unit told students the economy ends up "128 times" larger over the long run. That figure comes from a quick classroom shortcut, and the real number is closer to 114. We relabeled both growth figures as approximate, so on the very first page a rough estimate isn't read as a hard measurement.
2
Unit 2 · Markets and Coordination
Students were pointed at the wrong reading
Shipped
The unit's required reading didn't actually cover supply and demand, the unit's core topic, while the reading that did was marked optional. We swapped them. A student who simply follows the instructions now lands on the pages the unit and its assignment are built around.
3
Unit 3 · Thinking Like an Economist
A cause-and-effect explanation that was simply wrong
Shipped
Explaining why trade-offs get steeper as you do more of something, the unit said it was because workers are "roughly indifferent" between jobs, framing it as a matter of preference. The real reason is that people are better at some tasks than others. We corrected it, so students learn the actual mechanism instead of a plausible-sounding wrong one.
4
Unit 4 · Welfare and Government Policy
A garbled sentence that also fed a graded question
Shipped
The explanation of who really pays a tax was tangled enough that a student couldn't follow the logic, and that same logic showed up on a graded problem. We rewrote it plainly: because smokers keep buying and companies can adjust, smokers end up bearing most of a tobacco tax. The lesson now prepares students for the question that tests it.
5
Unit 5 · Gains from Trade
The headline example claimed a result it never showed
Shipped
The unit's main trade example asserted that specializing "produces more," but never showed the before-and-after to prove it. We added the numbers (working alone: 8 and 8; specialized and trading: 12 and 12), so students can see the gain rather than take it on faith.
6
Unit 6 · Market Analysis
A worked example whose own numbers didn't agree
Shipped
In the sugar case study, the stated benefit to growers (about $1 to $1.5 billion) didn't match what the case's own method produces (about $2.5 to $2.7 billion), roughly double. We reconciled the figure, so the numbers a student is asked to work through actually add up.
7
Unit 7 · Midterm Exam
The review proposed a change, and we said no
Declined
This is the gate working. The review suggested copying the exam's rules (timing, weight, proctoring) onto the unit page. We declined: those rules live on the exam itself, and keeping a second copy just lets the two drift out of sync. Not every suggestion is an improvement, and the process leaves that call to a person.
8
Unit 8 · Measuring the Economy
Students were graded on a term the lessons never defined
Shipped
The unit's graded essay asks about "real GDP per capita," but the worked examples only ever calculated total GDP, never the per-person version. We added a one-line definition (total GDP divided by population), closing the gap between what's taught and what's tested.
9
Unit 9 · Why Countries Grow
A factual error about how South Korea grew
Shipped
Explaining South Korea's rise, the unit credited democracy alongside property rights and free markets. In fact Korea grew fastest from 1961 to 1987 under an authoritarian government and only became a democracy afterward. We separated the two, so students get the history right.
10
Unit 10 · Money, the Fed, and Exchange Rates
An "always works" claim that doesn't always work
Shipped
The unit described one of the Fed's interest-rate tools as a hard floor the market rate can never fall below. It actually holds only for banks; other lenders can and do go lower, which is why the Fed built a second tool. We added the qualifier, turning an overstated rule into an accurate one.
11
Unit 11 · Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply
A shortcut that led students to the wrong conclusion
Shipped
A central relationship was explained as "purely accounting," which would lead a student to think arithmetic alone decides how much the economy produces and at what prices. We added a sentence: the accounting only sets the range of possibilities; where the economy actually lands depends on supply and demand together.
12
Unit 12 · Fiscal Policy and the Two-Crisis Capstone
A worked concept whose numbers didn't line up
Shipped
The unit explained the spending "multiplier" with figures that didn't fit together from one passage to the next (at one point implying it could be several dollars, elsewhere less than one). We made it consistent: roughly one to two dollars in a slack economy, less than one near full employment. Students now get a single coherent picture.
13
Unit 13 · Cumulative Review
The review unit contradicted itself right before the final
Shipped
Reviewing fiscal policy, the unit blamed "crowding out" for weakening the 2009 stimulus, even though the same unit notes interest rates were at zero, where that effect basically can't occur. We requalified it to the real reason (households used the money to pay down debt), so students aren't handed a contradiction on the eve of the exam.
14
Unit 14 · Final Exam
Read like every other unit, and confirmed clean
Declined
The review reads exam units too. Its only suggestions here were cosmetic, like restating the exam logistics on the unit page, and we left them out for the same reason as the midterm: one source of truth for the exam rules. The point is that the review confirms when a unit is already doing its job rather than inventing work.

A review like this finds the things that quietly cost students, fixes them, and proves the fix held. It runs the same way on any course in the catalog.
